# Review — Perfect Price Trap

## Summary
This was not a thesis error. This was an execution error.

Originally planned premarket limit buys at:
- **STOCK_A: $80**
- **STOCK_B: $50**

Before the open, lowered them to:
- **STOCK_A: $78**
- **STOCK_B: $48**

Intraday lows later reached:
- **STOCK_A: $78.5**
- **STOCK_B: $50.2**

That means the original plan likely would have filled, while the revised "perfect price" plan missed both entries entirely. A macro headline then triggered a sharp rebound.

## What actually went wrong
The mistake was not "failing to predict the rebound." The mistake happened earlier:
- A reasonable first limit already existed
- The first limit was moved lower to chase a prettier entry
- Participation rights were exchanged for a low-probability ideal fill

This was a classic **perfect-price trap**.

## Core lesson
**Do not move a reasonable first buy limit lower just because you feel price may go lower.**

If the original first tranche remains valid inside the planned accumulation zone:
- **Keep the first tranche**
- If desired, **add a second lower tranche**
- Do **not** replace the first tranche with a greedier price

## Structural diagnosis
This error is a disguised form of FOMO.

Not the usual "I need to buy now" FOMO, but:
- fear of buying at a less-than-perfect price
- fear of not looking smart
- fear of missing the lowest possible print

In practice, this creates a worse outcome:
- no position
- no participation
- emotional frustration when the stock rebounds without you

## Correct execution model
Wrong:
- STOCK_A $80 → $78
- STOCK_B $50 → $48

Right:
- STOCK_A $80 first tranche, $78 second tranche
- STOCK_B $50 first tranche, $48 second tranche

The first tranche secures participation.
The second tranche is for deeper weakness.

## Rule extracted
1. **First-tranche integrity**: never cancel or downgrade a valid first tranche just to chase a more perfect price.
2. **Second-tranche option**: if you want a better average, add a lower second tranche rather than moving the first one.
3. **Participation over perfection**: a good planned fill is superior to an ideal fill that never happens.

## Final line
This miss was not bad luck.
It was the direct consequence of optimizing for the prettiest possible trade instead of a repeatable one.
